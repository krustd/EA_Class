\subsection{Conceptual to Relational Mapping Logic}

The transition from the conceptual model in Seminar 4 to the relational database schema relies on standard mapping rules:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Entity Mapping}: Business objects (e.g., Customers, Products, Stores) map to separate relation tables, with a unique identifier acting as the Primary Key (PK).
    \item \textbf{1:1 Relationships}: The customer-membership association (D2) is mapped by adding a unique foreign key `CustomerId (FK)` to the `MembershipAccounts` table. This enforces that a member is tied to exactly one customer.
    \item \textbf{1:N Relationships}: Relationships such as `DigitalOrders -- OrderItem` (F1) and `Store -- StoreStaff` (F4) are represented by placing the parent's primary key (`OrderId` or `StoreId`) as a Foreign Key (FK) in the child table. For order items, the primary key is composite `(OrderId, ItemNr)` to support multiple lines per order.
    \item \textbf{M:N Relationships}: Relational databases cannot store multi-valued lists directly. Therefore, many-to-many associations are resolved by creating **bridge tables** that contain composite primary keys formed by the primary keys of both participating tables:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item `RecipeMaterials` connects `RecipeStandards` and `RawMaterials` (S3) and records the specific `Dosage` of each ingredient in a drink.
        \item `StoreMaterials` tracks the current inventory level (`StockLevel`) of each raw material within a specific store (S5).
        \item `MemberCampaigns` records the coupons distributed to members (D4).
        \item `OrderCampaigns` links promotion campaigns to digital orders to trace which discounts were applied to a specific purchase (D5).
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

% =================== BUSINESS SCENARIOS ===================
\section{Business Scenarios Supported by the Relational Schema}

To demonstrate the completeness of the relational database design, we trace the SQL joining logic for three core retail scenarios.

\subsection{Scenario 1: Mobile Order and Pickup Fulfilment}
When a customer logs in, uses a coupon, submits a coffee order, and picks it up at a store:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SQL Joins}: 
    `Customers` join `MembershipAccounts` on `CustomerId` $\rightarrow$ join `MemberCampaigns` and `PromotionCampaigns` on `MemberId` / `CampaignId` $\rightarrow$ join `DigitalOrders` on `MemberId` $\rightarrow$ join `OrderItems` and `Products` on `OrderId` / `ProductId` $\rightarrow$ join `FulfilmentTasks` on `OrderId` $\rightarrow$ join `Stores` on `StoreId`.
    \item \textbf{Data Flow}: Traces customer acquisition, coupon application, beverage customization, barista preparation time, and store collection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scenario 2: R\&D Product Launch and Margin Auditing}
When R\&D releases a new beverage series and corporate analysts evaluate actual gross margins vs. standard ingredient costs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SQL Joins}: 
    `Products` join `RecipeStandards` on `ProductId` $\rightarrow$ join `RecipeMaterials` on `RecipeId` $\rightarrow$ join `RawMaterials` on `MaterialId` $\rightarrow$ join `Suppliers` on `SupplierId`. 
    Additionally, join `Products` to `OrderItems` on `ProductId` to pull actual transaction discounts.
    \item \textbf{Data Flow}: Allows the system to compare the standard cost (sum of ingredient dosages $\times$ raw material purchase cost) against the actual net revenue (item price minus custom discounts) to audit gross margins.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scenario 3: Store Inventory Replenishment}
When a store's stock of coffee beans falls below the safety threshold, triggering an automated replenishment order:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SQL Joins}: 
    `Stores` join `StoreMaterials` on `StoreId` $\rightarrow$ join `RawMaterials` on `MaterialId` $\rightarrow$ join `Suppliers` on `SupplierId`.
    \item \textbf{Data Flow}: The system queries `StoreMaterials` where `StockLevel` $<$ safety threshold, aggregates required quantities, groups them by `SupplierId`, and submits purchase orders to suppliers.
\end{itemize}

% =================== CONCLUSION ===================
\section{Conclusion}

This report successfully mapped the strategic and conceptual architecture of Luckin Coffee to a relational database implementation. 

First, describing Row 1 of the Zachman Framework established the strategic context by outlining what data is critical, how processes operate, where nodes are located, who the stakeholders are, when events occur, and why specific strategies are chosen. 

Second, the relational database design translated the conceptual objects from Seminar 4 into 18 primary and bridge tables. By defining primary keys, foreign keys, and structural mapping rules, the database schema supports critical business operations, including mobile ordering, recipe control, inventory management, and promotional auditing. The schema diagram visually establishes the structural integrity of the database, providing a robust blueprint for enterprise architecture and database programming.
